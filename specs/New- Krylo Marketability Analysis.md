Perceived State

Using your supply chain example:

Guest query: “Is a supply chain becoming constrained?”

Google can retrieve:

articles about supply chains

recent disruptions

But KRYLO's job is different.

1. Perceived State
KRYLO first answers:

"Given available observations, what does the environment appear to be doing?"

Not:

"What happened?"

Not:

"What does someone think?"

But:

"What condition is emerging?"

Example:

Supply Chain Environment

Perceived State:

Constraint formation appears to be increasing.

Supporting observations:

freight capacity tightening

lead times extending

inventory buffers declining

supplier concentration increasing

Confidence:

high / medium / low

Dissonance:

where signals disagree

This is the state of the environment as currently perceived through evidence.

The query creates a condition model.

"What information would have the highest decision value given this detected condition?"

Critical signals
emerging bottlenecks

capacity changes

supplier dependencies

logistics pressure

Leading indicators
purchasing patterns

construction activity

hiring changes

commodity movements

Contradictions
news says "supply improving"

Opportunity/risk zones
who benefits?

who is exposed?

where is leverage forming?

The difference:

Google:

"Here are 10 million documents related to supply chains."

KRYLO:

"Given the current environmental condition, these are the observations most relevant to understanding what is changing."

The phrase that captures this:

KRYLO does not optimize retrieval. It optimizes relevance to the emerging state.

That is the right architectural question: how is that brokerage layer created?

The short answer: through an intermediate state model between the guest query and information retrieval.

KRYLO should not go:

Query → Search → Results
It should go:

Query
  ↓
Intent / Condition Resolution
  ↓
Perceived State Model
  ↓
Information Requirements
  ↓
Evidence Collection
  ↓
Signal Weighting
  ↓
Optimized Intelligence Surface
Using your supply chain example:

1. Guest query enters
"Is a supply chain becoming constrained?"

The system extracts:

Subject: supply chain

Condition: constraint

Temporal orientation: emerging/current

Question type: state assessment

It does not know why the user cares.

2. KRYLO creates the "state hypothesis"
Not a conclusion — a measurement frame.

Example:

Constraint State Model

To understand constraint, KRYLO knows it needs to observe:

availability

movement

capacity

demand pressure

external friction

This is where your ontology/evidence architecture matters.

3. The system brokers relevance
The question becomes:

"What observations have the highest information value for this state?"

Not:

"What documents mention supply chain?"

So evidence gets ranked by relevance to the condition.

For example:

High relevance:

freight utilization data

port congestion

supplier announcements

production interruptions

Lower relevance:

generic supply chain opinion pieces

old industry commentary

broad economic articles

4. Perceived State emerges
The system aggregates:

signal strength

source independence

confidence

recency

convergence

Result:

"Supply chain constraint conditions appear to be forming."

5. Optional observer lens
Only after the state exists:

teacher → educational explanation

parent → consumer impact

operator → business implication

investor → market implication

The "broker" is essentially a relevance arbitration engine.

That is where I think KRYLO's differentiation lives. It is not a better search engine. It is a system that brokers between:

human question → environmental condition → evidence → meaning

The hard part is that middle layer: defining what evidence matters for a condition, not just what evidence exists about a topic.

That maps very closely to your existing ideas around observation objects, evidence tiers, convergence scoring, and SES.
